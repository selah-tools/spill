# Vision Doc: Spill — Christian Conversation Prompt Generator

## Product vision

This product starts as a lightweight web experience that helps people move beyond shallow conversation into honest, faith-shaped dialogue.[cite:3] The initial vision is not a full party game; it is a simple, beautiful prompt generator that can be used in person, in small groups, on dates, in families, or in church contexts.[cite:3][cite:1]

**The name is Spill.** The invitation is simple: _"Wanna play Spill?"_

The long-term opportunity is a browser-native social game inspired by the room-based feel of Jackbox, but the first proof should be the prompt engine itself.[cite:89][cite:101] That approach reduces product risk by validating whether Christian-worldview prompts actually create meaningful engagement before adding multiplayer systems, voting flows, or realtime rooms.[cite:95][cite:101]

## Problem

Many Christians want better conversations, but most social settings drift toward either small talk or forced, awkward "deep" questions.[cite:3] A thoughtfully structured prompt experience can create a low-friction bridge from casual presence to honesty, encouragement, prayer, testimony, and scripture-centered reflection.[cite:3]

The source inspiration positions itself around real conversations of faith, movement beyond small talk, and a structured prompt ladder with multiple levels plus wildcards.[cite:3] That validates the core category, but the digital product should differentiate by being faster to access, easier to share, and eventually more playable in group settings.[cite:3]

## Strategic thesis

The thesis is: build the smallest useful product that helps people have better Christian conversations, then let that content system grow into a more interactive social format.[cite:85] The MVP should therefore optimize for low maintenance, simple deployment, and fast iteration rather than for maximal game mechanics.[cite:87][cite:85]

This direction also fits the user's broader preference for minimal-capacity, automation-heavy product paths instead of overbuilding complex systems too early.[cite:87] The prompt library and generation logic become the durable asset, while later realtime play becomes a delivery layer on top of that asset.[cite:87][cite:101]

## Product principles

### 1. Start with content, not rooms

The first product should answer one question well: "Can this app reliably surface a Christian conversation prompt that people actually want to use?"[cite:87] It should not begin with room codes, anonymous submissions, timers, moderation, or scoring.[cite:87]

### 2. Christian worldview, not generic self-help

Prompts should reflect a Christian worldview in tone and substance, including themes like honesty before God, prayer, repentance, encouragement, church life, mission, identity in Christ, suffering, gratitude, and scripture reflection.[cite:3] The app should feel spiritually grounded without becoming preachy, cheesy, or overly church-programmed.[cite:3]

### 3. Simple enough to spread

The best first-use experience is extremely short: choose context, choose depth, get one prompt.[cite:87] A person should be able to open it in a room and use it within seconds, without setup, account creation, or explanation.[cite:87]

**The test:** Can a 25-year-old say "Wanna play Spill?" and have friends immediately get it?

### 4. Learn selection before generation

Early intelligence should improve which existing prompt gets shown, not try to autonomously invent production prompts from sparse votes.[cite:95][cite:101] Contextual-bandit style selection is a better eventual fit than full RLHF-style generation because the system observes context, chooses one action, and receives limited reward feedback from that displayed option.[cite:95][cite:101]

## Target users

Primary users:

- Christian friends hanging out and wanting a better question than small talk.
- Small group leaders who need a quick conversation starter.
- Dating or engaged couples who want guided, faith-shaped discussion.
- Families or youth leaders who want discussion prompts that are safe but meaningful.

Secondary users:

- Church leaders who may later want custom packs or host-led group sessions.
- Content creators, retreat leaders, and ministries who may eventually use themed prompt sets.

## Product phases

| Phase   | Goal                  | Key capability                                                |
| ------- | --------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Phase 1 | Prove prompt value    | Solo/shared prompt generator at spill.cards                   |
| Phase 2 | Prove group usage     | Host-generated shared session link                            |
| Phase 3 | Prove social gameplay | Room code, synchronized prompts, optional anonymous responses |
| Phase 4 | Expand format         | Jackbox-style party flow with rounds, reveals, and voting     |

The correct first hill to climb is Phase 1.[cite:87] If the prompts themselves do not land, richer game structure will not rescue the product.[cite:87]

## Success definition

The MVP is successful if people repeatedly use it in real conversations, share prompts with others, and ask for more contexts or prompt packs.[cite:95] The first signs of pull are not revenue or polish; they are repeat generation, saved prompts, shared prompts, and positive ratings by context.[cite:95][cite:101]

## Long-term vision

Long term, this can become a family of faith-centered conversation products:

- A simple prompt generator for everyday use.
- Group session tools for leaders and hosts.
- A browser party game with shared screen and phones.
- Themed packs by audience, season, or spiritual need.

The enduring moat is not the UI alone; it is a trusted prompt system that consistently produces honest, helpful, theologically safe conversation moments.[cite:3][cite:95]
